Why This Paper Matters

Combinatorial optimisation, quantum annealers, and a hard physical limit

The promise

  • Combinatorial optimisation (scheduling, routing, circuit design, protein folding) is NP-hard in general.
  • Quantum annealers (D-Wave) encode such problems as interacting spins; the lowest-energy spin configuration is the optimum.

The catch

  • Each qubit can only interact with a small fixed set of physical neighbours.
  • If the problem needs a coupling that the hardware does not wire, it cannot be encoded directly. This is the connectivity restriction.

Problem Formulation

QUBO and the equivalent Ising model

QUBO (binary)

$$\hat{x} \;=\; \arg\min_{x \in \{0,1\}^n} \; x^{\top} Q \, x$$

$x$ is a length-$n$ binary vector. $Q_{ij}$ encodes the pairwise interaction between variables $i$ and $j$.

Ising (spins)

$$\hat{s} \;=\; \arg\min_{s \in \{-1,+1\}^n} \; s^{\top} A\, s + s^{\top} b$$

Spins in $\{-1,+1\}$ match physical qubit states. Conversion: $s = 2x - 1$, with $A = Q/4$ and $b = Q\mathbf{1}/2$.

Key hardware constraint. A non-zero entry $A_{ij}$ requires a physical coupling wire between qubits $i$ and $j$. The root of the connectivity problem.

The Standard Fix: Minor Embedding

What it is, and why it does not scale

What it is

Bundle several physical qubits into a chain that acts as one logical qubit. A chain-strength penalty in the Hamiltonian forces all qubits in a chain to agree, creating virtual long-range couplings.

Why it is painful

  • Finding the embedding is itself NP-hard, no efficient general algorithm.
  • Recomputed per instance, up to ten minutes of preprocessing.
  • Chains consume qubits: 5,000 physical $\rightarrow$ $\approx$ 150 logical for dense problems.
  • Long chains compete with the problem signal, reducing effective precision.

The Core Idea: Splitting the Coupling Matrix

Hardware-friendly part exact, remainder linearised

$$A \;=\; A_{Q} \;+\; A_{L}, \qquad E(s) \;=\; s^{\top}A_{Q}s \;+\; s^{\top}A_{L}s$$
$A_{Q}$, quadratic part. Edges already on the hardware ($M_{ij}=1$). Handled exactly by the annealer.
$A_{L}$, linearised part. Remaining edges. Approximated as a per-qubit bias (next slide).

Linearisation and Damping

Turning the leftover quadratic part into per-qubit biases

Step 1. Taylor linearisation of $A_L$

$$s^{\top} A_{L}\, s \;\approx\; (s^{(k)})^{\top} A_{L}\, s^{(k)} \;+\; \big(2 A_{L}\, s^{(k)}\big)^{\top} s \;+\; \text{const}$$

The right-hand side is linear in $s$: just a fixed bias per qubit. No new coupling wires needed.

Step 2. Damping term

$$\frac{\lambda}{2}\, \big\| s - s^{(k)} \big\|^{2}$$

Penalises jumps where the Taylor approximation would break down. On $\{-1,+1\}^{n}$, the squared norm is constant, so this also reduces to a per-qubit bias.

Weak monotonicity is proven whenever $\lambda \;\ge\; \|2 A_{L}\|$ (spectral norm).

The Full Iteration Step

One hardware-compatible subproblem per iteration

$$s^{(k+1)} \;=\; \arg\min_{s\in\{-1,+1\}^n} \; \underbrace{s^{\top} A_{Q}\, s}_{\text{exact}} \;+\; \underbrace{\big(2 A_{L}\, s^{(k)}\big)^{\top} s}_{\text{linearised bias}} \;-\; \underbrace{\lambda\, (s^{(k)})^{\top} s}_{\text{damping bias}}$$
Term 1. Exact quadratic part. Handled natively by the annealer's coupling wires after the random permutation.
Term 2. Linearised contribution of $A_L$. The vector $2 A_L s^{(k)}$ is fixed from the previous iterate, so this is a per-qubit bias.
Term 3. Damping bias, also linear in $s$. Pulls the new iterate toward $s^{(k)}$. Larger $\lambda$ = more conservative step.

Connection. Mathematically equivalent to proximal gradient descent on $\min f(s) + g(s)$ with $f(s) = s^{\top} A_L s$ and $g(s) = s^{\top} A_Q s + \mathbb{I}_{\{-1,+1\}^n}$. The main empirical competitor, LNLS, is the degenerate case where most variables are fixed.

Random Permutation & Algorithm

Spreading approximation error; choosing $\lambda$ efficiently

Why permute

A fixed split always linearises the same edges. A random permutation $P_k$ each iteration reshuffles which edges land on hardware:

$$A^{(k)}_{Q} = (P_k^{\top} M P_k)\, \odot\, A.$$

Every edge of $A$ gets the exact treatment some of the time; error spreads evenly.

Choosing $\lambda$

A variable only flips when its entry in the linear force vector changes sign. Only the midpoints between sorted $|L|$ entries give distinct iterates. Default: 15 candidates per iteration; keep the lowest-energy result.

Algorithm

  1. Initialise: random $s \in \{-1,+1\}^n$.
  2. Draw permutation $P_k$.
  3. Split $A$ into $A^{(k)}_Q$ and $A^{(k)}_L$.
  4. Compute the bias from $A^{(k)}_L$ and $s^{(k)}$.
  5. Sub-iterations over $\lambda$ candidates; keep best.
  6. Update $s$; repeat from step 2.
Two nested loops: outer over permutations, inner over $\lambda$ values. One hardware solve per inner step.

Weak Monotonicity Proof

The energy never increases when $\lambda$ is large enough

Theorem. If $\lambda \ge \|2 A_{L}\|$, then $E(s^{(k+1)}) \le E(s^{(k)})$ for every $k$.

Step 1. Surrogate overestimates the true energy

$$E^{(k)}(x) \;\ge\; E(x) \qquad \text{for all } x \in \{-1,+1\}^{n}.$$

Taylor remainder theorem: for $\lambda$ large enough, the linearisation+damping surrogate is a safe upper bound.

Step 2. Algorithm minimises the surrogate

$$E^{(k)}(s^{(k+1)}) \;\le\; E^{(k)}(s^{(k)}).$$

Step 3. Chain the inequalities

$$E(s^{(k+1)}) \;\le\; E^{(k)}(s^{(k+1)}) \;\le\; E^{(k)}(s^{(k)}) \;=\; E(s^{(k)}). \qquad \blacksquare$$
Convergence. Only $2^{n}$ binary vectors exist; non-increasing energy plus generic distinct values forces termination in finitely many steps.

Experimental Results

Spin glass, MQLib Max-Cut, and D-Wave Advantage hardware

Setup & datasets

  • D-Wave Neal SA solver, restricted to Pegasus connectivity (controlled benchmark).
  • Real hardware: D-Wave Advantage v6.4, 20 µs anneal, chain strength $1.001\times$.
  • Reg spin glass: 270 instances, sizes from 10 upward (some exceed 150).
  • MQLib Max-Cut: 909 instances, 500–1000 variables.

Key findings

  • Small instances: embedding works, but its 10 min preprocessing dwarfs everything else (Fig. 6b, right).
  • Large instances: embedding fails; splitting matches LNLS $m\approx 30$.
  • Dense graphs favour splitting; sparse graphs favour specialised heuristics.
  • Damping ablation (922 instances): when damping wins, it wins by a larger margin (296 vs 164 decisive cases).
  • D-Wave hardware: works for $\pm 1$ weights; fails on float / wide-range integers (precision).

Limitations, Future Work & Conclusions

Where the method falls short, where it goes next, and the bottom line

Limitations

  • Converges to a local minimum, not the global one.
  • D-Wave precision limits floats and wide-range integer weights.
  • No reliable predictor of when splitting beats LNLS.
  • $\lambda$ selection is heuristic.
  • Tested only on Pegasus topology.

Future work

  • Validate the SA extension (Sec. 3.8) to escape local minima.
  • Principled $\lambda$ schedules instead of the sign-change heuristic.
  • Predictor models for when splitting beats LNLS.
  • Test on newer topologies (Zephyr) and gate-based annealers.

Conclusions

  • First splitting + linearisation for discrete QUBO on hardware-restricted annealers.
  • No NP-hard preprocessing; scales beyond what embedding can fit.
  • Weak monotonicity proven for $\lambda \ge \|2 A_L\|$.
  • Validated on D-Wave Advantage for $\pm 1$ weights.
Bottom line. Splitting trades the (already non-existent) global optimum of minor embedding for the ability to scale beyond what current hardware can embed, with zero NP-hard preprocessing and a proven convergence guarantee.
